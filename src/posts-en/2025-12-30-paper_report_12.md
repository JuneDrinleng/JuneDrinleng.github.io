## 1 Timeperceiver: An Encoder-Decoder framework for generalized time series forecasting

> Lee, J. & Lee, H. TIMEPERCEIVER: An encoder-decoder framework for generalized time-series forecasting.Traditional time series models often focus on how to better optimize the encoded input to predict and train the model. However, this makes prediction and training a separate or secondary focus. The author of this article developed a TIMEPERCEIVER encoding-decoding prediction framework and tightly combined it with an effective training strategy. Experimental results also show that the architecture exhibits SOTA performance.

![image-20251230094826997](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/JuneDrinleng/JuneDrinleng.github.io/main/assets/img/image-20251230094826997.png)

The standard time series prediction paradigm is the model after the contract is signed, that is, if a sequence has a total of 10 points, the information of the first 6 points is used to predict the information of the next four points. But in this framework, the author splits these 10 points into two parts, L and J, where L is the data used for prediction, and J is the content that needs to be predicted. It is generated by random split, and the final training uses L's data to predict J's data.Specifically, the original time series is divided into several patches, and then random split is performed to obtain $X_L$ and $X_J$. $X_L$ is combined with the encoding of indices and serialized before being put into encoding. At the same time, the indices of part J are also input into the decoding block after passing through the TPE+CPE module and Sequentialize. The encoding results are combined to make subsequent predictions, and then $\hat{X_J}$ can be predicted.

## 2 Synthetic sequence--Generation of symbolic data of time series basic model> Wang, W., Wu, K., Li, Y. B., Wang, D. & Zhang, X. Synthetic Series-Symbol Data Generation for Time Series Foundation Models. Preprint at https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2510.08445 (2025).

Related research on time series analysis has made great progress in the past, however, challenges such as scarcity and imbalance of training data still hinder their development. This paper develops a symbolic method for generating time series data and mathematical formulas, utilizing a contrastive learning mechanism. This is because the authors believe that sequential symbolic data generation and pre-training mechanisms have the potential to overcome data scarcity and improve task performance.

![image-20251230103023176](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/JuneDrinleng/JuneDrinleng.github.io/main/assets/img/image-20251230103023176.png)The premise of this idea is that time series data are low-dimensional samples of complex systems, and the essence of complex systems emerges through quantification through simple symbolic dynamics. This creates a bridge between time series and symbolic representation.

![image-20251230103240032](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/JuneDrinleng/JuneDrinleng.github.io/main/assets/img/image-20251230103240032.png)

Specifically, this article includes two parts: data generation and symbolic learning of time series. Data generation is to randomly sample the sampled time series data through the model, and then input it into the symbolic network to obtain the corresponding mathematical symbolic expression behind the time series. Finally, the symbolic expression is used to generate the corresponding time series.

On the model, this is done by inputting both time series and symbolic expressions into the model to perform comparative learning.

## 3 A review of machine learning for single particle tracking: methods, challenges and biophysical insights (intensive reading)> Zhang, C. *et al.* Review of machine learning for single-particle tracking: Methods, challenges, and biophysical insights. *Chem. Biomed. Imaging* https://doi.org/10.1021/cbmi.5c00146 (2025) doi:[10.1021/cbmi.5c00146](https://doi.org/10.1021/cbmi.5c00146).

### 3.1 Background and Summary

SPT is already a mature tool for monitoring the movement of single molecules or particles in living cells or other biological systems. Traditional methods of analyzing SPT data include fitting through MSD or decomposing trajectories into simple parameters through HMM, which all require prior knowledge.

Relatively speaking, machine learning can automatically parse meaningful features from trajectory data, and this parsing is often obtained by algorithms from high-dimensional spaces full of noise, which is often difficult for human analysts to do.Currently, the applications of machine learning in single particle tracing include particle detection, trajectory reconstruction, diffusion pattern classification, segmentation of motion states, high-precision positioning in two or three dimensions, noise suppression, and uncertainty quantification to improve analysis reliability.

### 3.2 Imaging methods of single particle tracing technology

There are two main schools of imaging methods in single particle tracing technology, namely imaging in two dimensions and imaging in three dimensions.

#### 3.2.1 2D-SPT

First, let’s look at 2D-SPT. The most common ones are epifluorescence microscopy (fluorescence microscope, the most common, EFM), total internal reflection fluorescence microscopy (TIRFM), highly inclined and laminated optical sheet (HILO) microscopy, and light sheet fluorescence microscopy (LSFM, light sheet fluorescence microscope).![image-20251230110444247](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/JuneDrinleng/JuneDrinleng.github.io/main/assets/img/image-20251230110444247.png)

The most common fluorescence microscope is widely used, but the problem is that EFM usually has a low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) due to its large excitation volume, thus limiting the single molecule positioning accuracy.

To overcome the low signal-to-noise ratio of ordinary fluorescence microscopy, TIRFM was developed by creating an evanescent excitation field at the interface between media with different refractive indices. **The spatial positioning accuracy is on the order of tens of nanometers, and the time resolution is on the order of milliseconds. **But the problem is that it is inherently limited by imaging depth.

HILO microscopy circumvents the axial limitations of TIRFM by using a slightly tilted excitation beam located just below the critical angle. It uses a thinner excitation plate to penetrate deeper into the sample (~10μm).

On this basis, light sheet LSFM further enhances the imaging depth and contrast by illuminating the sample with a transversely oriented optical sheet (Figure 1b), and clearly separates the excitation and detection paths.All of the above are 2D imaging methods, and they also obtain 2D single particle translation trajectories. However, the inability to detect axial motion (z direction) may lead to misinterpretation of three-dimensional particle dynamics as restricted lateral motion or reduced diffusion rate.

#### 3.2.2 3D-SPT

Since 2D-SPT faces the problem of three-dimensional particle motion being projected into two dimensions, developing 3D SPT methods is now being widely studied.

The most intuitive idea is to directly shoot the entire image stack and then reconstruct the three-dimensional trajectory - image-based tracking. 70 axial information is obtained by stacking a series of 2D images to generate a 3D image and linking particle positions across frames. This is shown in Figure a below, but this results in poor temporal resolution because the entire image stack has to be taken each time.

![image-20251230111351417](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/JuneDrinleng/JuneDrinleng.github.io/main/assets/img/image-20251230111351417.png)In order to overcome this problem, a three-dimensional trajectory reconstruction algorithm based on the point spread function (PSF) has been applied. Specifically, through a device similar to the one in Figure b, a cylindrical lens is used to introduce controlled optical aberration to generate an asymmetric PSF whose shape changes with the axial displacement, and then reconstruct the three-dimensional information based on the axial displacement change. Related research has included DH-PSF, SLMs, etc. This method can achieve (positioning progress of 25nm in the x-y scale, positioning precision of 50nm in the z direction). The limitation is the optics complexity (calibration challenges) that comes with higher brightness illumination> Upon insertion of the cylindrical lens, the beam experiences unequal convergence along the two orthogonal axes, causing the PSF to appear as ellipses of different orientations and eccentricities above and below the focal plane. This shape variation enables nanometer-scale inference of the emitter’s axial position, thereby achieving high-precision 3D localization (Figure 2b).Another solution is biplane imaging, which has two channels: a spectroscope and a prism. When particles move in the z direction, the relative position and intensity ratio of the image points will change, so that the z position information (robust) can be calculated through this change. Under good signal-to-noise ratio conditions, millisecond-level spatial accuracy is high.

The limitations of these solutions are the optical design complexity of imaging, as well as the trade-off between photon intensity and image depth.

**In addition to the above-mentioned 3D reconstruction SPT trajectory method based on image information**, Closed-loop feedback 3d-spt dynamically maintains particles within the imaging volume through real-time adjustment, thereby improving the axial tracking range and time resolution.
