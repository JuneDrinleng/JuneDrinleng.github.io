## 1 Osmotic Pressure-Induced Membrane Formation and Morphological Transformation

> Ref: X. Qiao *et al.*, *Journal of the American Chemical Society* (2025), doi:[10.1021/jacs.5c01581](https://doi.org/10.1021/jacs.5c01581).

This is a paper in *Journal of the American Chemical Society*, where phospholipids form the membrane, with Su-Am and PMEDAB-Chol (a cationic polymer condense) inside. These two components can form liquid-liquid phase separation, and adding phospholipids leads to membrane-bound droplets:

<img src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/JuneDrinleng/JuneDrinleng.github.io/main/assets/img/02182a618f2296bcb4bc68b0ce19765.png" alt="02182a618f2296bcb4bc68b0ce19765" style="zoom: 50%;" />

These droplets exhibit different behaviors under dextran-water or PEG external conditions.

First, the droplets are characterized. The membrane uses red fluorescent molecules, while the internal components are labeled with green fluorescence:

![8c5940ce9993c34713b4573e121b5a1](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/JuneDrinleng/JuneDrinleng.github.io/main/assets/img/8c5940ce9993c34713b4573e121b5a1.png)

Additionally, basic information like FSC-A is measured using a flow cytometer.

The authors then add water to the droplets, reducing the internal osmotic pressure, and observe the formation of smaller droplets inside:

![1530c46a70a7c5ecf004baada7df1cc](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/JuneDrinleng/JuneDrinleng.github.io/main/assets/img/1530c46a70a7c5ecf004baada7df1cc.png)

As seen in Figure b, water molecules form smaller droplets over time, which gradually dissolve as time progresses ![images_large_ja5c01581_0003](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/JuneDrinleng/JuneDrinleng.github.io/main/assets/img/images_large_ja5c01581_0003.jpeg).

The current fluorescence appears to suggest catalytic activity within the droplets, producing fluorescent intermediates.

When PEG is added, the membrane ruptures and re-forms:

## 2 Redox-Controlled Self-Assembly of Supramolecular Components

When multiple supramolecular components are mixed, they often interfere with each other. Here, the authors use the reduced precursors of supramolecular monomers in a membrane reactor, and based on different redox rates, sequentially obtain self-organized monomers (oxidized precursors). This prevents component A from self-assembling while component B is still being formed, avoiding mixed self-assembly.

> Ref: S. Patra, S. Dhiman, S. J. George, *Angew Chem Int Ed*. **64**, e202500456 (2025).

![0f2483f32efa98754519199ba8ceca5](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/JuneDrinleng/JuneDrinleng.github.io/main/assets/img/0f2483f32efa98754519199ba8ceca5.png)![De

This is the schematic diagram of the paper, showing the entire reaction occurring within a DOPC membrane. Two pairs of self-assembly precursors are used: NDI and OEt-NDI. Both can be oxidized to form self-assembly monomers, but their self-assembly morphologies differ—one forms self-assembly droplets, and the other forms fiber-like structures.

![image-20250617121558660](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/JuneDrinleng/JuneDrinleng.github.io/main/assets/img/image-20250617121558660.png)

Here, adding an ethoxy group to NDI changes the potential, increasing the overall potential and extending the reaction time.

![image-20250617121800249](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/JuneDrinleng/JuneDrinleng.github.io/main/assets/img/image-20250617121800249.png)

I think this approach is feasible, using DOPC-like substances as the reactor membrane for internal self-assembly or sequential self-assembly.
