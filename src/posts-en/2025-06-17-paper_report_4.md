## 2 Redox-controlled self-ordering assembly of supramolecular components

When a variety of supramolecular molecules are mixed together, mutual interference between assemblies often occurs. Here, the reduced precursors of the supramolecular monomers are placed in the membrane reactor, and then the self-organized monomers (oxidized reduced precursors) are obtained successively according to different redox rates. This will lead to the self-assembly of component A while component B has not yet been generated, thus avoiding the doping of the two self-assemblies.

> Ref: S. Patra, S. Dhiman, S. J. George, *Angew Chem Int Ed*. **64**, e202500456 (2025).

![0f2483f32efa98754519199ba8ceca5](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/JuneDrinleng/JuneDrinleng.github.io/main/assets/img/0f2483f32efa98754519199ba8ceca5.png)![De

This is the schematic diagram of this article. You can see that the entire reaction is carried out in a membrane composed of DOPC. Two pairs of self-assembly precursors are selected, namely NDI and OEt-NDI. Both can be oxidized to obtain self-assembly monomers, but their self-assembly forms are different. One forms self-assembled droplets and the other forms fibers.

![image-20250617121558660](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/JuneDrinleng/JuneDrinleng.github.io/main/assets/img/image-20250617121558660.png)

This is actually due to the addition of an ethoxy group to NDI, which changes the potential. The overall potential increases and the reaction time also becomes longer.

![image-20250617121800249](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/JuneDrinleng/JuneDrinleng.github.io/main/assets/img/image-20250617121800249.png)

I feel like this is really possible. Using a substance like DOPC as the reactor membrane, self-assembly/sequential self-assembly occurs inside.
